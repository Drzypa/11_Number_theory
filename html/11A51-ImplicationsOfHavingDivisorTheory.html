implications of having divisor theory


The existence of a divisor theory restricts strongly the of an integral domain, as is seen from the following propositions.

Proposition 1.  An integral domain 𝒪 which has a divisor theory  𝒪*→𝔇,  is integrally closed in its quotient field.

Proof.  Let ξ be an element of the quotient field of 𝒪 which is integral over 𝒪.  Then ξ satisfies an equation

ξn+α1⁢ξn-1+…+αn=0 (1)

where  α1,…,αn∈𝒪.  Now, we can write  ξ=ϰλ  with  ϰ,λ∈𝒪,  whence (1) may be written

ϰn=-α1⁢λ⁢ϰn-1-α2⁢λ2⁢ϰn-2-…-αn⁢λn. (2)

Let us make the antithesis that ξ does not belong to 𝒪 itself.  Then  λ∤ϰ  and therefore we have for the corresponding principal divisors  (λ)∤(ϰ).  We infer that there is a prime divisor factor 𝔭 of (λ) and an integer k≧0 such that

𝔭k∣(ϰ),𝔭k+1∤(ϰ),𝔭k+1∣(λ).

By the condition 2 of the definition of divisor theory (http://planetmath.org/DivisorTheory), the right hand side of the equation (2) is divisible by

𝔭(k+1)+(n-1)⁢k=𝔭k⁢n+1.

On the other side, the highest power of 𝔭, by which the divisor (ϰn) is divisible, is 𝔭k⁢n.  Accordingly, the different sides of (2) show different divisibility by powers of 𝔭.  This contradictory situation means that the antithesis was wrong and thus the proposition has been proven.

Proposition 2.  When an integral domain 𝒪 has a divisor theory  𝒪*→𝔇,  then each element of 𝒪* has only a finite number of non-associated (http://planetmath.org/Associates) factors (http://planetmath.org/DivisibilityInRings).

Proof.  Let ξ be an arbitrary non-zero element of 𝒪.  We form the prime factor presentation of the corresponding principal divisor (ξ):

(ξ)=𝔭1⁢𝔭2⁢⋯⁢𝔭r

This is unique up to the ordering of the factors;  r≧0.  Then we form of the prime divisors 𝔭i all products having k factors (0≦k≦r) and choose from the products those which are principal divisors.  Thus we obtain a set of factors of (ξ) containing at most (rk) elements.  All different principal divisor factors of (ξ) are gotten, as k runs all integers from 0 to r, and their number is at most equal to

∑k=0r(rk)=2r

(see 5. in the binomial coefficients).  To every principal divisor , there corresponds a class (http://planetmath.org/EquivalenceClass) of associate factors of ξ, and the elements of distinct classes (http://planetmath.org/EquivalenceClass) are non-associates.  Since ξ has not other factors, the number of its non-associated factors is at most 2r.
