Basel problem derivation


The basis for this derivation is the assumption that the properties of finite polynomials hold true for infinite series. First, consider the Taylor series expansion of the sine function:

sin⁡x=∑n=0∞(-1)n(2⁢n+1)!⁢x2⁢n+1=x-x33!+x55!-x77!+⋯

Dividing through by x, we get:

sin⁡xx=1-x23!+x45!-x67!+⋯

The roots of the function sin⁡x/x occur at x=n⁢π, where n=±1,±2,±3,…. Let us assume that we can express this infinite series as a product of linear factors given by its roots, just as we do for finite polynomials:

sin⁡xx =(1-xπ)⁢(1+xπ)⁢(1-x2⁢π)⁢(1+x2⁢π)⁢(1-x3⁢π)⁢(1+x3⁢π)⁢⋯
=(1-x2π2)⁢(1-x24⁢π2)⁢(1-x29⁢π2)⁢⋯.

If we multiply out this product and collect all the x2 terms, we get:

-(1π2+14⁢π2+19⁢π2+⋯)=-1π2⁢∑n=1∞1n2

From the original infinite series of sin⁡x/x the coefficient of x2 is -1/3!. Equating both expressions gives:

-16=-1π2⁢∑n=1∞1n2
∑n=1∞1n2=π26

This was the method Euler used to solve the Basel problem. There are more rigorous and modern derivations, but this one is a simple, straight forward, and easily understood one.
