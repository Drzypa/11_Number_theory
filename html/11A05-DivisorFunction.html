divisor function


In the parent article there has been proved the formula

σ1⁢(n)=∑0⁢<d∣⁢nd=∏i=1kpimi+1-1pi-1

giving the sum of all positive divisors of an integer n; there the pi’s are the distinct positive prime factors of n and mi’s their multiplicities.

It follows that the sum of the z’th powers of those divisors is given by

σz⁢(n)=∑0⁢<d∣⁢ndz=∏i=1kpi(mi+1)⁢z-1piz-1. (1)

This complex function of z is called divisor function (http://planetmath.org/DivisorFunction).  The equation (1) may be written in the form

σz⁢(n)=∏i=1k(1+piz+pi2⁢z+…+pimi⁢z) (2)

usable also for  z=0.  For the special case of one prime power the function consists of the single geometric sum (http://planetmath.org/GeometricSeries)

σz⁢(pm)= 1+pz+p2⁢z+…+pm⁢z,

which particularly gives m+1 when pz=1, i.e. when z is a multiple of 2⁢i⁢π/ln⁡p.

A special case of the function (1) is the τ function (http://planetmath.org/TauFunction) of n:

σ0⁢(n)=∑0⁢<d∣⁢n1=∏i=1k(mi+1)=τ⁢(n)

Some inequalities

σm⁢(n)≥nm2⁢σ0⁢(n) for m=0, 1, 2,…
σ1⁢(m⁢n)>σ1⁢(m)+σ1⁢(n) ∀m,n∈ℤ
σ1⁢(n)≤n+12⁢σ0⁢(n)
n+n<σ1⁢(n)<6π2⋅n⁢n
