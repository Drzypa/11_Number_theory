Theorem (Generalisation of Wilson’s Theorem).

For all integers 1≤k≤p-1,p∈P⇔(p-k)!(k-1)!≡(-1)k(modp)

Proof.

If p is a prime, then:

(p-k)!≡(p-1)!⁢(p-1)¯⁢⋯⁢(p-k+1)¯≡(p-1)!⁢(-1)¯⁢⋯⁢(1-k)¯==(p-1)!⁢(-1)k-1⁢(k-1)!¯(modp),

and since (p-1)!≡-1(modp) (Wilson’s Theorem, simply pair up each number — except p-1 and 1, the only numbers in ℤp which are their own inverses — with its inverse), the first implication follows.

Now, if p∣(p-1)!(k-1)!-(-1)k, then p∈ℙ as the opposite would mean that p=a⁢b, for some integers 1<a,b<p, and so p would not be relatively prime to (p-1)!⁢(k-1)! as the initial hypothesis implies. ∎
