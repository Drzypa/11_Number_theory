Theorem 1.

There are an infinite number of primes congruent to 3(mod4).

Proof.

Choose any prime p≡3(mod4); we find a prime of that form that exceeds p.

N=(22⋅3⋅5⋅7⁢⋯⁢p)-1

Clearly N≡3(mod4), and thus must have at least one prime factor that is also ≡3(mod4). But N is not divisible by any prime less than or equal to p, so must be divisible by some prime congruent to 3(mod4) that exceeds p. ∎

Theorem 2.

There are an infinite number of primes congruent to 1(mod4).

Proof.

Given N>1, we find a prime p>N with p≡1(mod4). Let p be the smallest (odd) prime factor of (N!)2+1; note that p>N. Now

(N!)2≡-1(modp)

and therefore

(N!)p-1≡(-1)(p-1)/2(modp).

By Fermat’s little theorem, (N!)p-1≡1(modp), so we have

(-1)(p-1)/2≡1(modp)

The left-hand side cannot be -1, since then 0≡2(modp). Thus (-1)(p-1)/2=1 and it follows that p≡1(mod4). ∎

Note that the variant of Euclid’s proof of the infinitude of primes used in the proof of Theorem 1 does not work for Theorem 2, since we cannot conclude that an integer ≡1(mod4) has a factor of the same kind.
