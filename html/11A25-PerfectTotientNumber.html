perfect totient number


An integer n is a perfect totient number if

n=∑i=1c+1ϕi⁢(n)

, where ϕi⁢(x) is the iterated totient function and c is the integer such that ϕc⁢(n)=2.

A082897 in Sloane’s OEIS lists the first few perfect totient numbers: 3, 9, 15, 27, 39, 81, 111, 183, 243, 255, 327, 363, 471, 729, 2187, 2199, 3063, 4359, 4375, etc. It can be observed that many of these are multiples of 3 (in fact, 4375 is the smallest one that is not divisible by 3) and in fact all 3x for x>0 are perfect totient numbers.

Furthermore, 3⁢p for a prime p>3 is a perfect totient number if and only if p=4⁢n+1, where n itself is also a perfect totient number. Mohan and Suryanarayana showed why 3⁢p can’t be a perfect totient number when p≡3mod4. In regards to 32⁢p, Ianucci et al showed that if it is a perfect totient number then p is a prime of one of three specific forms listen in their paper. It is not known if there are any perfect totient numbers of the form 3x⁢p for x>3.
